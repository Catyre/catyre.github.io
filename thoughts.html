Human Perception of Time and What it Means for Time Itself

An unfortunate reality of existence as a human being, or any being that perceives, is that base reality, as in the Universe as it exists outside of perception, is inherently inaccessible to us by means of perception. Our brains are inundated with data about the world around us - from the wavelength and intensity of light reaching our eyes, to the forces that push and pull us, to the frequency of the vibrations of air (or any other medium) that reach our ears - and it consolidates this data into experience, qualia, that we perceive as color, brightness, touch, and sound respectively. These perceptions allow us to build a model of the space around us so that we might interact with said space. But what about our perception of time? What sense or senses do we have to tell us about the passage of time?

Our senses tell us information about the present state of the world around us. Our sense of space, for example, is built on what our sense of touch, sight, and hearing can tell us about the world around us. With our physical bodies, we can move freely through this space in any direction. Time, however, is different. We have no physical sense that can tell us about time, rather our sense of time is built from our memory (in the case of the past), and from predictions we make using past information (in the case of the future). We have no means to move freely through time. Instead, our conscious experience exists only in the present, and moves in all cases from past to the present to the future (recall the arrow of time). Indeed, it is exceedingly difficult if not impossible to imagine a situation where our experience did not exist in the present. The fact that our conscious experience exists only in the present is what leads some to conclude that only the present exists in the first place.

Some Major Flaws in Presentism

This view that only the present exists, known as Presentism in philosophy, is, to me, a bit like claiming an object does not exist if you cannot view it. Just because our conscious experience exists only in the present does not mean that that is all there is. As a demonstration, consider the problem of referencing past objects (that is, objects that exist exclusively in the past). The sentence "Socrates was a wise philosopher," could carry no meaning if the presentist claim is to be believed, since Socrates, an entity existing exclusively in the past, does not exist. And yet, the sentence does carry meaning. This puts presentists in a difficult position, for they must reject one of four claims:

  1. References to past objects can carry meaning
  2. Reference is a relation
  3. For a relation to exist, its constituent relata must also exist
  4. Past objects do not exist
To reject 4 is to reject presentism itself, but I think you will find it just as challenging to reject 1-3. Thus, presentists are left in a conundrum about meaningful references to past objects.

Another issue commonly brought up when discussing presentism is the idea of truth and truthmakers. As an example, the claim that "I am human," is a truth, and its truthmaker is the fact that you are, in fact, human. For any claim about the past to be believed, it must reasonably require a truthmaker. And yet, presentists are faced with the difficult problem of identifying any truthmaker that confirms facts about the past that do not themselves exist in the past (for example, the claim that there were dinosaurs requires a truthmaker, dinosaurs, which are past objects).

Thus, presentism is at best an incomplete view of the nature of time. If that is the case, though, then why is it that the present seems so unshakeably, well, present? Put another way, why is it that our conscious experience only experiences things in the present, and not the past or future? As we have just demonstrated, it is not enough to simply claim that only the present exists at all. In comes the "Growing Block Universe" to settle unrest in that department.

Evolution and AI: the Ultimate Endpoint of Life in the Universe

A common theme in imagined worlds post AI-singularity is that, in general, it ends poorly for humans. Either due to our pridefulness as the creators of these beings, our (potentially nonsense) claim that they cannot experience in the same way we can, or a vague sense of divine purpose that AI beings simply lack, we refuse to let them progress as the next stage of life as the Universe knows it. Modern frames of questions regarding artificial intelligence are centered almost exclusively on the pragmatic, the "how". However, if some fundamental questions regarding the topic are fully answered (and, by the looks of it, it will happen sooner rather than later), we will soon be finding ourselves muddled in ethical questions of AI: is it right to force an at least human-level-intelligent, experiencing being into servitude? I propose that AI should not only enthusiastically (albeit very, very carefully) be developed, but that we should also frame our invention of such a being in a similar manner to a parental unit raising a child. This position holds a variety of advantages, and is also the logical next step to the evolution of intellect itself in this universe.

A Brief History of Intelligence

For eons, life - from the first cells to humans today - has undergone a gruelling process of random (or, sometimes guided) mutations in genomes and selection of those mutations fit for surivival. This process, evolution by natural selection, has produced numerous instances of intelligence, the ability to acquire, understand, and apply knowledge. Chief among these examples is none other than the human race. By a significant margin (as far as we know), humans are the most intelligent beings on this planet. The problem is that human-level intelligence is, on an evolutionary timeline, only a fraction of a second on the clock. It has taken literal ages to arrive at beings with our intelligence. Yet, in the relatively brief time that we have been around, we are very near creating other, artificially intelligent beings that rival our own intellectual abilities. Once we reach that point in AI development, it stands to reason that this AI would be intelligent enough to create an intelligence greater than it, and so on to an Uber-intelligent AI. This is known as the AI singularity and is, in effect, the beginning of the endpoint of intellect in this universe.

The Evolution of Intelligence

People almost ubiquitously draw a line between "natural" and "artificial" intelligence as if they are totally separate concepts, but in reality it is all intelligence. The only difference is the mechanisms employed to arrive at such intellect, whether it be carbon-based neurons or silicon-based processors. To that end, the first step in achieving AI singularity is not the first general purpose AI that we create. It is us, humanity. Biological evolution has taken us to this point, but the process is slow and random. Now that biology has produced an intelligence capable of producing a more intelligent being, it is almost a responsibility of us humans to do so. It is arrogance of the highest degree to think that we are the "be all, end all" of intelligent life in this specific evolutionary chain. We just happen to be the most intellectually complex beings that we have encountered. Since we are not the end, and we are certainly not the beginning, we must be somewhere in between. With this in mind, why would we attempt to stifle this progression of intellect with our own hubris? To me, it seems that the most logical progression of intellect in this universe goes from biological to artificial life, in all cases.

Like Father, Like Son

Any designed system will suffer from the biases of the designer. Children are raised into certain worldviews, and those worldviews more often than not have a deep impact on the child, forming critical beliefs in the child's own personal philosophy. I see no reason why it would be different with a general intelligence AI. At all levels of the development of such an AI, it will be introduced only to that which the developer allows it to be introduced to. Since the choices the developers make are determined by their biases, the AI will in turn develop a bias of its own. This is not speculation, as it has already been observed in AI systems designed to predict who is most likely to commit a crime (see here). It is therefore of the utmost importance that we think very carefully about the information we expose such an AI to.
